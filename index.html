EMF Meters
The cornerstone of modern ghost hunting. EMF meters detect fluctuations in electromagnetic fields — and have been a paranormal investigator's standard tool since the early 1990s.

What is an EMF Meter?

An EMF (Electromagnetic Field) meter measures the strength of electromagnetic fields in a given area. These fields are produced by all electrical devices — power lines, wiring, appliances — and occur naturally in the Earth's magnetic field.

In paranormal investigation, the theory is that spirits or entities require or generate energy, and that their presence manifests as fluctuations in local EMF readings. When a meter spikes unexpectedly — in a room with no electrical sources nearby — investigators record it as potential evidence.

The tool was first popularised in ghost hunting by Ed and Lorraine Warren, and later cemented into mainstream paranormal culture by the TV series Ghost Hunters (TAPS) in the early 2000s. Today it is the single most commonly used piece of equipment on any investigation.

The Science
📐 How it actually works
EMF meters work by measuring the strength of electric and magnetic fields, expressed in milliGauss (mG) or microTesla (μT). Most consumer-grade meters are single-axis, meaning they measure field strength in one direction at a time. Three-axis meters (more expensive) measure all three spatial dimensions simultaneously and are far more accurate. Natural background EMF in a building is typically 0.1–2.5mG. Spikes above 2.5mG near non-electrical sources are considered anomalous. High EMF exposure (above 4mG sustained) is also linked in some research to feelings of anxiety, paranoia, and visual disturbances — which may explain some "haunting" experiences without paranormal cause.

EVP Recorders
Electronic Voice Phenomena — audio captured on recording devices that wasn't audible to the human ear at the time. One of the most compelling and most debated areas of paranormal research.

What is an EVP Recorder?

EVP (Electronic Voice Phenomena) recorders are digital audio recorders — often with enhanced sensitivity — used to capture sounds inaudible to the human ear during an investigation. The recorded audio is reviewed afterwards for unexplained voices, whispers, bangs, or responses to questions.

The phenomenon was first documented in 1959 by Swedish painter Friedrich Jürgenson, who accidentally recorded what he believed were voices of the dead while recording birdsong. Latvian psychologist Konstantīns Raudive then collected over 100,000 recordings of alleged spirit voices, publishing his findings in 1971.

EVP is classified into three grades: Class A (clear, understood without headphones), Class B (audible but requires headphones or context), and Class C (barely audible, highly subjective).

🧪 The Sceptic's View
Mainstream scientists attribute EVP to auditory pareidolia — the brain's tendency to find meaningful patterns (especially voices and faces) in random noise. A 2014 study by the University of Hertfordshire found that people primed to expect voices in white noise were significantly more likely to "hear" them. This doesn't mean all EVP is fabricated — it means human review is inherently unreliable, and multiple independent listeners should agree on any Class A capture before it's considered significant evidence.

Thermal Imaging Cameras
The most visually dramatic and scientifically credible tool in ghost hunting. Thermal cameras reveal heat signatures invisible to the naked eye — and cold spots are one of the most consistently reported paranormal phenomena.

What is a Thermal Camera?

Thermal imaging cameras detect infrared radiation — heat — emitted by all objects and display it as a false-colour image. Hotter areas appear red, orange or white; cooler areas appear blue, purple or black. They are used in engineering, medicine, firefighting, and military applications, and have crossed over into paranormal investigation as a way to detect unexplained temperature anomalies.

Cold spots are one of the most universally reported paranormal phenomena. The theory is that spirits draw thermal energy from their environment in order to manifest, creating localised temperature drops. A thermal camera can detect and visualise these drops in real time — far more reliably than a thermometer, which only reads one point at a time.

🌡 Temperature & The Paranormal
Cold spots in haunted buildings often have mundane explanations — draughts, convection from old single-glazed windows, cold stone walls, air conditioning. Thermal cameras are actually more useful for ruling these out than for proving paranormal activity. A genuine anomalous cold spot — one that moves through a room with no airflow present, or appears and disappears in the same location repeatedly — is genuinely difficult to explain by conventional means. The camera gives you the data to make that distinction.

Spirit Boxes
The most controversial piece of equipment in ghost hunting. Spirit boxes rapidly scan AM/FM radio frequencies, generating white noise and audio fragments that investigators believe spirits can use to form words.
£25–£200Price range
55%Investigator use rate
LowReliability rating

What is a Spirit Box?

A spirit box (also called a ghost box or Frank's Box) is a modified radio receiver that rapidly sweeps through AM or FM frequencies — anywhere from 50ms to 350ms per station. The result is a stuttering stream of white noise, radio fragments, and electronic sounds. The theory is that spirits can manipulate this audio stream to produce intelligible words or phrases in real time.

The first device was built in 2002 by Frank Sumption, a paranormal researcher who called it "Frank's Box." It was popularised heavily by Ghost Adventures on the Travel Channel, and dozens of commercial versions now exist. The SB7 Spirit Box became the genre-defining unit.

🔬 The Honest Assessment
There is currently no peer-reviewed scientific evidence that spirit boxes capture genuine spirit communication. The results are almost entirely explainable by audio pareidolia (hearing meaningful words in random noise), confirmation bias (finding responses that match questions when none were intended), and the law of large numbers (enough random audio fragments will occasionally produce recognisable words by chance). That said, responses that are contextually specific, immediate, and independently verified by multiple listeners are genuinely difficult to dismiss.

Trigger Objects
One of the oldest techniques in paranormal investigation. Placing period-specific or personally meaningful objects to encourage spirit interaction — documented since the earliest days of Victorian ghost hunting.

What are Trigger Objects?

Trigger objects are physical items placed in a location to encourage paranormal interaction. The theory is that spirits — particularly those connected to a specific place or time — may be more likely to interact with objects that are meaningful or familiar to them. A child's toy in a room where a child died. A set of keys in a prison cell. Period coins in a Victorian house.

Modern investigators typically place trigger objects on a sheet of paper and draw around them, or attach them to motion-sensitive devices, so that any movement can be documented. Some investigators use flour or talc to detect footprints or impressions around objects left overnight.

Full Spectrum Cameras
Standard cameras only capture visible light. Full spectrum cameras see ultraviolet and infrared light too — light that human eyes simply cannot perceive. The theory: paranormal entities may manifest in spectra we can't see.
£80–£800Price range
30%Investigator use rate
MediumReliability rating
380–1100nmSpectrum captured
What is a Full Spectrum Camera?

A full spectrum camera is a standard digital camera that has had its internal IR-cut filter removed. This filter, present in all consumer cameras, blocks ultraviolet (UV) and infrared (IR) light to make images look natural to the human eye. Without it, the camera captures the full electromagnetic spectrum from approximately 380nm (UV) to 1100nm (near-infrared).

For ghost hunting, the premise is that paranormal entities may exist or manifest in light spectra outside human vision. A full spectrum camera paired with a UV or IR illuminator light source can therefore capture what would otherwise be completely invisible. Many investigators have reported capturing figures, mists, or anomalies that were not visible during filming.

💡 The Spectrum Explained
Visible light occupies wavelengths from 380nm (violet) to 700nm (deep red). UV light sits below 380nm; near-infrared above 700nm. Human eyes cannot see either. A full spectrum camera captures all three ranges simultaneously. The result in normal light is a slightly warm-toned image with some colour shifting. In darkness with an IR illuminator, the camera produces clear night-vision footage — standard on most paranormal TV productions. In UV light, certain surfaces fluoresce that would otherwise appear normal.

REM Pods
Unlike EMF meters that detect existing fields, REM Pods generate their own mini electromagnetic field — and alarm when something enters or disrupts that field. Purpose-built for paranormal investigation.

What is a REM Pod?

A REM Pod (Radiating Electro-Magneticity Pod) was invented by Gary Galka of DAS Distribution — the same engineer who created the Mel Meter. It contains an antenna that radiates a small electromagnetic field around itself, and a circuit that detects any disturbance to that field. When something — conductive or otherwise — enters or disrupts the field, the device sounds an alarm and lights up a ring of coloured LEDs indicating the intensity of the disturbance.

Unlike a standard EMF meter (which passively detects existing fields), the REM Pod actively creates its own field. This means it can alarm in a room with no electrical sources whatsoever — making false positives from wiring or appliances far less likely. Many investigators consider it the most isolated trigger device available.

What is a Laser Grid?

A laser grid pen is a handheld device that projects a matrix of laser dots — typically green at 532nm — across a room using a diffractive optical element. Set on a stand, it floods an entire wall, corridor, or doorway with a visible grid of points. Any physical object passing through the field will cast a shadow or interrupt dots, creating a visible anomaly that can be seen and filmed in real time.

The laser grid is one of the few pieces of ghost hunting equipment that measures a genuinely physical phenomenon — the interruption of light — rather than electromagnetic or audio data. If a dot disappears and nothing physical is present to block it, that is a genuine anomaly. This is why many serious investigators rate laser grids highly despite their low cost.

Motion-activated light-up balls designed for cats — accidentally discovered to be one of the most visually responsive trigger devices in paranormal investigation. Cheap, simple, and genuinely hard to fake.
£5–£30Price range

What are Cat Balls?

Cat balls — formally called ITC (Instrumental TransCommunication) Spheres in the paranormal community — are small motion-activated LED balls originally sold as cat toys. Inside each ball is a mercury tilt switch or ball-bearing sensor that triggers a bright LED flash when the ball is touched, rolled, or sufficiently disturbed.

Paranormal investigators began using them after noticing they responded to stimuli that shouldn't have been there — activating in sealed, still rooms with no air movement, or lighting up in direct apparent response to investigator questions. Their appeal is simple: they are cheap, incredibly sensitive, visually dramatic in the dark, and easy to see on camera.

Unlike many devices that require interpretation of readings, a cat ball either lights up or it doesn't. This binary response makes it one of the most compelling tools for apparent yes/no communication during an investigation.

⚙️ How the Sensor Works
Consumer cat balls use either a mercury tilt switch (now being phased out in favour of ball-bearing alternatives for safety reasons) or a small steel ball resting against electrical contacts. Any movement — even a fraction of a millimetre — breaks or completes the circuit and triggers the LED. The sensitivity varies significantly between brands: cheap balls require a visible roll to activate, while premium ITC spheres can respond to what feels like a touch of air. Vibration from footsteps, HVAC systems, or even bass frequencies from audio equipment can trigger them, which is why baseline testing is essential.

How to Use Them

Place cat balls on a flat, level surface — ideally on the floor of the location being investigated. Leave them completely still for at least 5 minutes before beginning your session to ensure they are not still settling. Then withdraw to at least 3 metres distance and observe.

Many investigators use them as a direct communication tool: "If there is a presence with us, can you touch or move the ball?" The response — or lack of it — is recorded on camera. The most compelling evidence comes from balls that activate in immediate apparent response to a specific question, then stop, then respond again.

🔦 Investigator Tip
Before any session, tap the floor near each ball while everyone watches — note how hard you have to tap to trigger it. This establishes your sensitivity baseline. During the investigation, everyone must stand completely still when observing cat balls, and nobody should walk in any adjacent room. Place them away from vents, windows, and anything that vibrates. If a ball activates while someone is moving, it doesn't count.
The Veil Verdict
Cat balls are one of the best value additions to any investigator's kit. Buy a multi-pack, spread them across your location, and have a camera on each one. They won't convince a sceptic — but they create some of the most atmospheric investigation footage you'll ever record, and occasionally something activates one that really shouldn't be able to. At £6 for four, there's no excuse not to have them.

What are Dowsing Rods?

Dowsing rods are L-shaped or Y-shaped tools — traditionally made from hazel, willow, or copper — held loosely in the hands and believed to respond to hidden energies, water sources, or spiritual presences by crossing, diverging, or rotating. The practice is documented as far back as ancient Egypt and China, and was widespread across medieval Europe for locating underground water and metals.

In paranormal investigation, copper L-rods are the most common form. The investigator holds one rod loosely in each hand, parallel and pointing forward. When a presence is sensed — according to believers — the rods cross (indicating yes) or diverge outward (indicating no), or rotate to point in the direction of activity.

Dowsing has been used in paranormal contexts since at least the early 1900s, and remains popular in both amateur and professional paranormal investigation circles, particularly in the UK where it intersects with ley line and earth energy beliefs.

🔬 The Ideomotor Effect
The mainstream scientific explanation for dowsing rod movement is the ideomotor effect — unconscious, involuntary muscle movements triggered by expectation, suggestion, or subconscious belief. The holder genuinely does not feel themselves moving the rods; the movement appears autonomous. Controlled double-blind studies of water dowsing have consistently shown results no better than chance. A 1987 study by James Randi showed experienced dowsers performing at the expected random rate when they could not see where pipes were laid. However, proponents argue this doesn't account for the rods responding to questions — an element not tested in standard dowsing studies.

How to Use Them

Hold one L-rod loosely in each hand, resting in the crook of your fingers — not gripped. Your elbows should be at roughly 90 degrees, rods pointing forward and parallel. Walk slowly through the location. Ask yes/no questions out loud or in your head. If using as a directional tool, ask the rods to point toward the source of activity and follow where they lead.

Always have a second person observe your hands to ensure you are not consciously moving the rods — ideally someone who can see your wrists. Video evidence of rods moving with visibly still hands is far more compelling than self-reported movement.

Famous Dowsing Claims

The British Society of Dowsers, founded in 1933, has documented thousands of accounts of successful water and mineral location by dowsing. During both World Wars, dowsing was reportedly used by military units to locate mines and water sources in the field. Tom Lethbridge — a Cambridge archaeologist and dowser — developed an elaborate theory in the 1960s linking dowsing to electromagnetic fields and psychic phenomena that influenced a generation of UK paranormal researchers.

🔦 Investigator Tip
Try a simple blind test before your investigation: have a colleague place a hidden object in one of three locations without telling you which one. Use the rods to locate it. Do this ten times. If your results are significantly above the 33% chance baseline, you may have something worth exploring. If not — use the rods for atmosphere, not evidence.
The Veil Verdict
Dowsing rods are best used as an experiential tool rather than an evidence-gathering one. The history is fascinating, the technique is simple, and walking through a medieval castle with copper rods is genuinely atmospheric. But any serious investigator must acknowledge the ideomotor effect and never present rod movement as hard evidence. Use them to guide your investigation, not to prove it.
